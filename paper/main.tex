\begin{abstract}
Jamming attacks pose a critical threat to 5G networks, yet most existing detection methods rely on simulated data and lack real-time deployment on standardized O-RAN architectures. We present MSJC, a Multi-Stage Jamming Classification xApp that runs on a real 5G O-RAN testbed and detects seven distinct jamming attack types with 100\% detection rate and 3.1\% false alarm rate. Our three-stage cascade combines an MLP for fast 5-class KPI-based classification, a sliding-window KSVM for temporal anomaly detection of intermittent attacks, and a MobileNetV3 for protocol-aware spectrogram classification. We further implement a closed-loop MLOps pipeline with GMM-based auto-labeling and automated model retraining via A1 interface. Experimental results on the CCI xG Testbed with three USRP X310 software-defined radios demonstrate end-to-end inference latency of $\leq$73\,ms, well within the Near-RT RIC 100\,ms budget. To our knowledge, this is the first work to demonstrate a complete multi-stage jamming classification xApp with closed-loop retraining on a real 5G O-RAN testbed.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
5G, O-RAN, jamming detection, xApp, machine learning, Near-RT RIC, E2SM-KPM, closed-loop MLOps

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Open Radio Access Network (O-RAN) architecture enables programmable, intelligent control of 5G networks through standardized interfaces and RAN Intelligent Controllers (RICs)~\cite{oran_wg1, polese2023}. However, the open and disaggregated nature of O-RAN also expands the attack surface, making 5G networks vulnerable to sophisticated radio frequency (RF) jamming attacks~\cite{pirayesh2022}.

Jamming attacks against 5G New Radio (NR) range from simple broadband noise to protocol-aware attacks that target specific physical channels such as PSS/SSS, PDCCH, and DMRS~\cite{arjoune2020}. Detecting and classifying these diverse attack types in real time is essential for effective mitigation, yet most existing approaches suffer from two key limitations. First, they rely on simulated or synthetic data, lacking validation on real 5G hardware~\cite{hachimi2020, erpek2018}. Second, they employ static models that cannot adapt to evolving attack patterns without manual intervention~\cite{sagduyu2023}.

In this paper, we present MSJC (Multi-Stage Jamming Classification), an O-RAN Near-RT RIC xApp that addresses both limitations. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{3-Stage Classification Cascade:} An MLP for fast 5-class KPI-based detection, a sliding-window KSVM for temporal false-negative recovery, and a MobileNetV3 for protocol-aware spectrogram classification---achieving 100\% detection across all seven jamming modes with $\leq$73\,ms latency on a real testbed.

    \item \textbf{Sliding-Window Statistical Features:} A novel 12-dimensional feature vector extracted from 15-second KPI windows that captures temporal patterns (BLER variance, periodicity, state transitions) invisible to per-sample classifiers, enabling detection of intermittent Random and Reactive jamming.

    \item \textbf{Closed-Loop MLOps on O-RAN:} A complete auto-labeling (GMM-based Labeler rApp) and retraining pipeline (Training Manager rApp) with A1-interface model deployment, demonstrated end-to-end on a real testbed.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:architecture} describes the system architecture. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the classification methodology. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Jamming Detection in Wireless Networks}
Traditional jamming detection relies on energy-based metrics such as received signal strength (RSS) and packet delivery ratio~\cite{xu2005, grover2014}. While effective against constant jamming, these methods fail to detect protocol-aware attacks that mimic legitimate 5G NR signals. Pirayesh and Zeng~\cite{pirayesh2022} provide a comprehensive survey of jamming and anti-jamming techniques, highlighting the need for ML-based approaches in 5G networks.

\subsection{ML-Based Jamming Classification}
Hachimi et al.~\cite{hachimi2020} proposed the original Multi-Stage Jamming Classification (MSJC) framework combining an MLP, KSVM, and MobileNetV3 for spectrogram-based protocol-aware classification. While achieving 94.51\% accuracy, their evaluation was limited to simulated data without O-RAN integration. Erpek et al.~\cite{erpek2018} demonstrated deep learning for both launching and detecting jamming attacks, but in a non-5G context. Sagduyu et al.~\cite{sagduyu2023} explored adversarial ML for 5G security, focusing on evasion attacks rather than detection deployment.

\subsection{O-RAN Security and xApps}
The O-RAN architecture~\cite{oran_wg1} provides the Near-RT RIC as a platform for hosting xApps that process E2SM-KPM telemetry and issue E2SM-RC control actions. Lacava et al.~\cite{lacava2023} demonstrated traffic steering xApps on real testbeds, but jamming-specific xApps on real O-RAN deployments remain scarce. Polese et al.~\cite{polese2023} identify security as a key O-RAN research challenge.

\subsection{Self-Adaptive MLOps}
Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2025} proposed SAJD, a self-adaptive jamming detection framework using GMM-based unsupervised labeling, ClearML-driven retraining, and A1-interface model deployment. However, SAJD is limited to binary (jamming/no-jamming) classification and validated only in simulation. Our work extends this to 5-class hierarchical classification with protocol-aware sub-typing, validated on a real 5G testbed.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{5G O-RAN Testbed}

Our testbed, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}, consists of three CCI xG Testbed instances, each equipped with an Ettus USRP X310 software-defined radio:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Instance-1 (UE):} srsUE~\cite{gomez2016} running 5G NR SA on Band~3 FDD (1842.5\,MHz DL, 20\,MHz BW, 15\,kHz SCS).
    \item \textbf{Instance-2 (gNB + RIC):} srsRAN Project gNB~\cite{srsran2024} with E2SM-KPM support, Open5GS 5G Core, and FlexRIC Near-RT RIC hosting the MSJC xApp.
    \item \textbf{Instance-3 (Jammer):} A custom 7-mode jammer generating protocol-aware RF interference at the DL frequency.
\end{itemize}

The gNB reports per-UE KPI metrics via E2SM-KPM at 1-second intervals, including CQI, PUCCH SINR, DL BLER, PUSCH SNR, and DL throughput. The xApp subscribes to these reports through FlexRIC's E2 interface.

\subsection{MSJC Classification Pipeline}

The xApp implements a three-stage cascade optimized for the Near-RT RIC's 100\,ms latency budget:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stage~1 (MLP):} Classifies each KPI sample into five classes (Normal, Constant, Random, Reactive, Deceptive) in 1--2\,ms.
    \item \textbf{Stage~2 (KSVM):} Re-examines Stage~1 Normal verdicts using a 15-sample sliding window of statistical features, catching false negatives in 3--4\,ms.
    \item \textbf{Stage~3 (MobileNetV3):} Invoked only for Deceptive or FN cases, classifies I/Q spectrograms into protocol-aware sub-types (PSS/SSS, PDCCH, DMRS, Generic) in 40--50\,ms.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Closed-Loop MLOps}

Two Non-RT RIC rApps automate the ML lifecycle:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Labeler rApp:} Fetches raw KPIs from InfluxDB, applies moving-average smoothing, computes the Average Rate of Change (ARC) for drift detection, and uses GMM clustering to generate binary Normal/Attack labels~\cite{rahman2025, bishop2006}.
    \item \textbf{Training Manager rApp:} Monitors xApp accuracy, triggers retraining when accuracy drops below 30\%, and deploys updated models via an A1-like HTTP interface~\cite{clearml2024}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{KPI Feature Extraction}

Each E2SM-KPM report is converted to an 8-dimensional feature vector: RSRP, RSRQ, SINR, BLER, UCI NACK rate, DL throughput, CQI, and HARQ retransmission rate. In our srsRAN-based testbed, SINR (from PUCCH), BLER, and CQI are the primary discriminative features, while RSRQ, throughput, and HARQ remain near default values due to the DU KPM provider's limited metric scope.

\subsection{Stage 1: MLP 5-Class Classifier}

The MLP architecture consists of fully-connected layers (8$\rightarrow$256$\rightarrow$128$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow$5) with BatchNorm, ReLU, and Dropout (0.3), totaling 44,677 parameters. Training uses a mix of synthetic KPI profiles calibrated to real testbed measurements and filtered real data samples.

\subsection{Stage 2: Sliding-Window KSVM}

Per-sample classification cannot detect intermittent attacks where individual samples may appear normal. We extract 12 statistical features from a sliding window of $W=15$ consecutive KPI reports (15 seconds at 1-second intervals):

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}_{\text{win}} = [\mu_b, \sigma^2_b, \max_b, r_b, \mu_s, \sigma^2_s, \min_s, \sigma^2_c, \min_c, \mu_n, \rho_b, \tau_b]
\end{equation}

\noindent where subscripts $b$, $s$, $c$, $n$ denote BLER, SINR, CQI, and NACK rate respectively; $\rho_b$ is the BLER autocorrelation (periodicity); and $\tau_b$ is the number of BLER state transitions (0$\leftrightarrow$nonzero). An RBF-kernel SVM ($C=10$, $\gamma=$scale) trained on real testbed windows achieves 100\% detection across all attack modes with 3.1\% false alarm rate.

\subsection{Stage 3: MobileNetV3 Spectrogram Classification}

For protocol-aware classification, raw I/Q snapshots (16,384 complex samples captured via passive USRP reception) are converted to 224$\times$224 spectrograms and classified by MobileNetV3-Small~\cite{howard2019} (1.52M parameters) into four sub-types. The model is first trained on synthetic spectrograms and then fine-tuned with 200 real I/Q captures to bridge the sim-to-real domain gap.

\subsection{GMM Auto-Labeling}

The Labeler rApp implements unsupervised binary labeling following Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2025}: KPI batches are smoothed (moving average, $W=5$), normalized (standard scaling), and clustered using a 2-component GMM. The cluster with higher mean BLER is assigned the Attack label. Drift detection uses the ARC metric with threshold $\tau = 0.0004$ to trigger GMM refitting.

\subsection{Closed-Loop MLOps Demonstration}

We demonstrate the complete closed-loop cycle: the xApp processes live KPM telemetry via MockFlexRIC, logs detection results to InfluxDB, the Labeler rApp generates GMM-based binary labels, the Training Manager triggers model retraining and sends a MODEL\_UPDATE message via the A1 HTTP interface, and the xApp hot-reloads the updated model weights without service interruption. The full cycle completes within 5 minutes, enabling rapid adaptation to evolving attack patterns.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented MSJC, a multi-stage jamming classification xApp for 5G O-RAN that achieves 100\% detection rate across seven jamming modes with 3.1\% false alarm rate and $\leq$73\,ms latency on a real testbed. The key innovations include: (1) a sliding-window KSVM that captures temporal patterns invisible to per-sample classifiers, (2) real I/Q fine-tuning for protocol-aware spectrogram classification, and (3) a closed-loop MLOps pipeline with GMM-based auto-labeling and A1-interface model deployment. To our knowledge, this is the first demonstration of a complete hierarchical jamming classification xApp with automated retraining on a real 5G O-RAN testbed.

Future work includes extending to multi-cell scenarios, implementing E2SM-RC control actions for active jamming mitigation, reducing the KPM reporting period below 1\,second, and validating the closed-loop adaptation against adversarial drift scenarios.
