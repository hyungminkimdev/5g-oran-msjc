\begin{abstract}
Jamming attacks pose a critical threat to 5G networks, yet most existing detection methods rely on simulated data and lack deployment on standardized O-RAN architectures. We present MSJC, a Multi-Stage Jamming Classification framework deployed on a real 5G O-RAN testbed that detects seven distinct jamming attack types---including protocol-aware attacks targeting PSS, PDCCH, and DMRS---with 100\% detection for six of seven modes (91.9\% for PDCCH) and 5.3\% false alarm rate. Our three-stage cascade combines: (1)~an MLP for fast 5-class KPI-based classification, (2)~a sliding-window KSVM that extracts 12-dimensional temporal statistics to detect intermittent attacks invisible to per-sample classifiers, and (3)~a MobileNetV3 for I/Q spectrogram-based protocol-aware sub-classification achieving 85\% accuracy across four sub-types. We further implement a closed-loop MLOps pipeline with GMM-based auto-labeling and automated retraining via the A1 interface. All results are validated via blocked temporal 5-fold cross-validation on 1,492 real testbed samples with zero sample overlap between train and test sets. Experiments on the CCI~xG Testbed with three USRP~X310 software-defined radios demonstrate end-to-end inference latency $\leq$23\,ms (p95), well within the Near-RT~RIC 100\,ms budget.
\end{abstract}

5G, O-RAN, jamming detection, xApp, machine learning, Near-RT RIC, E2SM-KPM, closed-loop MLOps

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Open Radio Access Network (O-RAN) architecture disaggregates the traditional RAN into programmable, multi-vendor components controlled by RAN Intelligent Controllers (RICs)~\cite{polese2023, abdalla2022}. While this openness enables innovation through xApps and rApps~\cite{santos2025, hoffmann2024}, it also expands the attack surface, making 5G networks increasingly vulnerable to sophisticated RF jamming~\cite{pirayesh2022, harvanek2024}. This threat is particularly acute for tactical and military 5G deployments operating in contested RF environments, where adversaries may employ protocol-aware electronic warfare (EW) techniques.

Modern jamming attacks against 5G New Radio (NR) extend well beyond simple broadband noise. Protocol-aware jammers can selectively target synchronization signals (PSS/SSS), control channels (PDCCH), or reference signals (DMRS), each producing distinct spectral signatures but minimal impact on aggregate KPI metrics~\cite{savadatti2024, shahid2024}. Detecting and classifying these diverse attack types in real time is essential for effective mitigation.

Existing ML-based jamming detection approaches suffer from two key limitations. First, most rely on simulated data without validation on real 5G hardware~\cite{hachimi2020, kryszkiewicz2023}. Jere et al.~\cite{jere2024} demonstrated Bayesian-assisted ML on a 5G NR testbed but did not integrate with O-RAN interfaces. Second, static models cannot adapt to evolving attack patterns; Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2025} proposed closed-loop retraining but limited to binary classification in simulation. Recent O-RAN-based approaches have begun addressing jamming on real testbeds~\cite{moore2025, bogucka2025, pazperez2025}, but none combine multi-class classification with protocol-aware sub-typing and automated retraining in a single framework.

In this paper, we present MSJC (Multi-Stage Jamming Classification), a framework implemented as a Near-RT RIC xApp for real-time inference with Non-RT RIC rApps for closed-loop MLOps, validated on the CCI~xG Testbed~\cite{dasilva2025}. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{3-Stage Classification Cascade:} Extending Hachimi et al.'s two-stage MLP$+$KSVM design~\cite{hachimi2020} from C-RAN to O-RAN, we add a third stage (MobileNetV3~\cite{howard2019}) for protocol-aware spectrogram classification, redesign the KSVM with sliding-window temporal features, expand from 4 to 7 jamming modes, and validate on real 5G hardware---achieving 100\% detection for six modes with 5.3\% false alarm rate and $\leq$23\,ms latency (p95), evaluated via blocked temporal cross-validation.

    \item \textbf{Sliding-Window Statistical Features:} A 12-dimensional feature vector extracted from 15-second KPI windows that captures temporal patterns (BLER variance, periodicity, state transitions) invisible to per-sample classifiers, enabling detection of intermittent Random and Reactive jamming.

    \item \textbf{Closed-Loop MLOps on Real O-RAN:} Adopting Rahman et al.'s SAJD architecture~\cite{rahman2025}, we implement GMM-based Labeler rApp and Training Manager rApp and extend them from binary detection to multi-class classification, with end-to-end validation on a real 5G testbed---the first such demonstration on actual hardware.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Jamming Attacks and Detection}

Pirayesh and Zeng~\cite{pirayesh2022} provide a comprehensive survey of jamming attacks and anti-jamming strategies across wireless networks, covering constant, reactive, and deceptive jamming types. Savadatti et al.~\cite{savadatti2024} propose a six-dimensional taxonomy specific to 5G jamming, while Shahid et al.~\cite{shahid2024} present a protocol-agnostic classification of physical-layer jamming techniques. Harvanek et al.~\cite{harvanek2024} survey 5G PHY-layer security threats including jamming, spoofing, and eavesdropping, noting the growing role of ML-based countermeasures.

\subsection{ML-Based Jamming Classification}

Hachimi et al.~\cite{hachimi2020} proposed a two-stage ML-based intrusion detection system for 5G Cloud-RAN (C-RAN), combining an MLP for 4-class jamming classification (constant, deceptive, random, reactive) with a Kernelized SVM (KSVM) as a second stage to reduce false negatives. Their system achieved 94.51\% accuracy on the WSN-DS dataset but was limited to: (i)~simulated data without real RF validation, (ii)~C-RAN architecture without O-RAN integration, and (iii)~per-sample classification that cannot capture temporal attack patterns. Our work extends Hachimi's two-stage MLP$+$KSVM concept to a three-stage cascade by adding MobileNetV3~\cite{howard2019} for protocol-aware spectrogram classification (PSS, PDCCH, DMRS), redesigning the KSVM with sliding-window temporal statistics, expanding to seven jamming modes, and deploying on a real 5G O-RAN testbed with E2SM-KPM telemetry. Jere et al.~\cite{jere2024} demonstrated Bayesian inference-assisted ML for near-real-time jamming detection on a 5G NR testbed, achieving AUC $>$0.988 with 180\,ms detection delay. However, their work does not integrate with O-RAN interfaces or support protocol-aware sub-classification.

\subsection{O-RAN-Based Security xApps}

The O-RAN architecture~\cite{polese2023} enables deploying security functions as xApps on the Near-RT RIC. Santos et al.~\cite{santos2025} and Hoffmann et al.~\cite{hoffmann2024} provide comprehensive guides to xApp development, documenting practical challenges. Kryszkiewicz and Hoffmann~\cite{kryszkiewicz2023} proposed statistical jamming detection via O-RAN using 2D~Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests, but only in simulation. Moore et al.~\cite{moore2025} demonstrated LSTM-based intrusion detection with secure slicing xApps on a real testbed. Bogucka et al.~\cite{bogucka2025} implemented jamming detection and MCS adaptation on an Amarisoft-based O-RAN testbed. Paz-P\'{e}rez et al.~\cite{pazperez2025} designed a four-xApp cascade (monitoring, detection, classification, mitigation) on FlexRIC. Reus-Muns et al.~\cite{reusmuns2024} demonstrated spectrogram-based radar detection using a YOLO xApp, validating I/Q spectrogram ML on O-RAN infrastructure. Dimou and Noubir~\cite{dimou2025} proposed ARGOS for rogue base station detection using VAE-based anomaly detection on the same srsRAN+FlexRIC stack used in our work.

\subsection{Closed-Loop MLOps for O-RAN}

Stephenson et al.~\cite{stephenson2023} demonstrated closed-loop AI-driven RAN control on an SDR testbed at MILCOM~2023. Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2025} proposed SAJD, a self-adaptive jamming \textit{detection} framework for O-RAN featuring a GMM-based Labeler rApp for unsupervised binary labeling, a Training Manager rApp with ClearML-driven retraining, and A1-interface model deployment. However, SAJD is limited to binary detection (jamming present/absent) without multi-class classification, and was validated only in simulation. Our work adopts Rahman's closed-loop MLOps architecture (Labeler rApp, Training Manager, A1 interface) and extends it from binary detection to 7-class classification with protocol-aware sub-typing, all validated on a real testbed. Marinova and Leon-Garcia~\cite{marinova2024} survey intelligent O-RAN architectures, highlighting AI/ML workflow automation as a key research direction. Kakani et al.~\cite{kakani2025} address the complementary problem of adversarial attacks against xApp ML models, while Rumesh et al.~\cite{rumesh2024} explore federated learning for anomaly detection in O-RAN digital twins.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{CCI xG Testbed}

All experiments are conducted on the CCI~xG Testbed~\cite{dasilva2025}, an O-RAN-compliant research platform at Virginia Tech. Our setup uses three instances:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Instance-1 (UE):} srsUE running 5G NR SA on Band~3 FDD (1842.5\,MHz DL, 20\,MHz BW, 15\,kHz SCS, 106~PRBs).
    \item \textbf{Instance-2 (gNB + RIC):} srsRAN Project gNB with E2SM-KPM support, Open5GS 5G Core, and FlexRIC Near-RT RIC hosting the MSJC xApp.
    \item \textbf{Instance-3 (Jammer):} A custom 7-mode jammer generating protocol-aware RF interference, with OFDM structure matched to the gNB (FFT=1536, CP=108).
\end{itemize}

Each instance is equipped with an Ettus USRP X310 SDR connected via 10G Ethernet (MTU~9000). The gNB reports per-UE KPI metrics via E2SM-KPM at 1-second intervals. Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the overall system architecture.

\subsection{Jammer Design}

Our custom jammer generates seven distinct attack modes, with OFDM symbol structure (FFT=1536, CP=108, 14~symbols/slot) matched to the target gNB to maximize interference effectiveness:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Constant:} Continuous broadband AWGN (amplitude~0.5, gain~0\,dB). Produces sustained BLER elevation ($>$0.7) and SINR degradation ($-$2.2\,dB).
    \item \textbf{Random:} Frame-based random-duty-cycle pulses (30\% duty, amplitude~0.6). Creates intermittent BLER spikes with high variance.
    \item \textbf{Reactive:} Slot-aware adaptive pulses (40\% duty, amplitude~0.6). Produces periodic BLER patterns detectable through autocorrelation.
    \item \textbf{Deceptive:} OFDM-like waveform mimicking legitimate transmissions (amplitude~0.6). Causes minimal KPI degradation (BLER $\approx$ 0).
    \item \textbf{PSS/SSS:} Narrowband attack on synchronization signals (6--12 center RBs, gain~10\,dB). Targets initial cell access.
    \item \textbf{PDCCH:} CORESET-targeted attack on control channels (slots~0--3 OFDM symbols, gain~5\,dB). Disrupts scheduling information.
    \item \textbf{DMRS:} Comb-pattern attack on demodulation reference signals (subcarrier spacing~4--6, gain~10\,dB). Corrupts channel estimation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MSJC Classification Pipeline}

The xApp implements a three-stage cascade optimized for the Near-RT RIC's 100\,ms latency budget:

\textbf{Stage~1 (MLP):} Classifies each 8-dimensional KPI vector into five classes (Normal, Constant, Random, Reactive, Deceptive) in 1--2\,ms. Constant, Random, and Reactive detections are confirmed immediately.

\textbf{Stage~2 (KSVM):} Re-examines Normal verdicts using a sliding window of $W=15$ samples (15~seconds). Twelve statistical features capture temporal patterns invisible to per-sample analysis. An RBF-kernel SVM decides Normal vs.\ Attack in 3--4\,ms.

\textbf{Stage~3 (MobileNetV3):} Invoked only for Deceptive detections or Stage~2 false-negative catches. Raw I/Q snapshots (16,384 complex samples via passive USRP reception) are converted to 224$\times$224 spectrograms~\cite{reusmuns2024} and classified into PSS/SSS, PDCCH, DMRS, or Generic Deceptive in 40--50\,ms.

\noindent Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the complete classification cascade and decision flow.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{MSJC three-stage classification cascade. Stage~1 classifies per-sample KPIs; Stage~2 re-examines Normal verdicts via sliding-window statistics; Stage~3 performs protocol-aware I/Q spectrogram classification. All paths complete within 23\,ms (p95).}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Closed-Loop MLOps}

Two Non-RT RIC rApps automate the ML lifecycle, adopting the SAJD closed-loop architecture proposed by Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2025} and extending it from binary detection to multi-class classification:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Labeler rApp:} Implements Rahman's GMM-based unsupervised labeling pipeline (moving average smoothing $\rightarrow$ ARC drift detection with $\tau = 0.0004$ $\rightarrow$ 2-component GMM clustering) to generate binary Normal/Attack annotations from raw KPI telemetry.
    \item \textbf{Training Manager rApp:} Monitors xApp accuracy via InfluxDB, triggers Stage~1 MLP retraining when accuracy drops below 30\%, and deploys updated models via an A1-like HTTP interface---extending Rahman's ClearML-based orchestration with a standalone fallback mode.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{KPI Feature Extraction}

Each E2SM-KPM indication message from the gNB is parsed and mapped to an 8-dimensional feature vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^8$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} = [\text{RSRP}, \text{RSRQ}, \text{SINR}, \text{BLER}, \text{NACK}, \text{Tput}, \text{CQI}, \text{HARQ}]
\end{equation}

\noindent where RSRP uses PUSCH SNR as a proxy, SINR is derived from PUCCH measurements, and CQI is automatically scaled from the E2SM-KPM range [0--15] to the accumulator range [0--50] used during training (a $\times$3.33 correction factor identified during real testbed integration). In practice, SINR, BLER, and CQI serve as the primary discriminators: Constant jamming causes SINR to drop from $+$5.6 to $-$2.2\,dB and BLER to spike to 0.75, while protocol-aware attacks (PSS, PDCCH, DMRS) leave all KPIs virtually unchanged (Table~\ref{tab:kpm_stats}).

\subsection{Stage 1: MLP 5-Class Classifier}

Following Hachimi et al.'s two-stage design~\cite{hachimi2020}, our Stage~1 uses an MLP as the first-pass classifier. We extend the original 4-class scheme to 5~classes (adding Deceptive as a catch-all for protocol-aware attacks) and replace raw I/Q features with 8-dimensional E2SM-KPM metrics. The architecture (8$\rightarrow$256$\rightarrow$128$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow$5) with BatchNorm, ReLU, and Dropout~(0.3) totals 44,677 parameters. Training uses a hybrid dataset: synthetic KPI profiles calibrated to real testbed measurements (500 per class) mixed with real labeled samples from the testbed (approximately 765 real samples per fold after filtering). Clean-looking attack samples (BLER~$<$~0.03 and SINR~$>$~3\,dB and CQI~$>$~48) are deliberately excluded from Stage~1 training, as these protocol-aware attacks are designed to be caught by Stage~2 and classified by Stage~3. To prevent train/test leakage, Stage~1 performance is evaluated via blocked temporal 5-fold cross-validation: each mode's time-series is split into five consecutive blocks, and each fold trains on four blocks' real data (plus synthetic) and tests on the held-out block.

\subsection{Stage 2: Sliding-Window KSVM}

Stage~1 classifies each KPI sample independently, which is effective for attacks causing persistent degradation (e.g., Constant) but fails to detect protocol-aware attacks that leave per-sample KPIs within normal ranges. To address this, Stage~2 re-examines all Stage~1 ``Normal'' verdicts by analyzing the \textit{temporal statistics} of a sliding window of $W=15$ consecutive KPI reports (15~seconds at 1\,Hz reporting rate).

We extract 12 statistical features from each window:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f} = [\mu_b, \sigma^2_b, b_{\max}, r_b, \mu_s, \sigma^2_s, s_{\min}, \sigma^2_c, c_{\min}, \mu_n, \rho_b, \tau_b]
\end{equation}

\noindent where $b$, $s$, $c$, $n$ denote BLER, SINR, CQI, NACK respectively. The features are designed to capture distinct temporal signatures:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BLER statistics} ($\mu_b$, $\sigma^2_b$, $b_{\max}$, $r_b$): Mean, variance, maximum, and spike ratio (fraction of samples with BLER~$>$~0.03). Constant jamming produces high $\mu_b$ ($>$0.35); Random/Reactive produce intermittent BLER spikes with high variance and transitions.
    \item \textbf{SINR/CQI statistics} ($\mu_s$, $\sigma^2_s$, $s_{\min}$, $\sigma^2_c$, $c_{\min}$): Capture signal quality degradation and variability. Protocol-aware attacks produce a distinct SINR signature ($\mu_s \approx 4.7$\,dB) compared to Normal traffic ($\mu_s \approx 5.6$\,dB), despite leaving BLER near zero. CQI variance ($\sigma^2_c$) further separates Normal traffic ($\sigma^2_c \approx 11$) from protocol-aware attacks ($\sigma^2_c \approx 1.2$), reflecting the natural scheduling-driven CQI fluctuations absent under attack.
    \item \textbf{Temporal dynamics} ($\rho_b$, $\tau_b$): BLER autocorrelation at lag-1 distinguishes Reactive (periodic, high $\rho_b$) from Random (aperiodic, low $\rho_b$) jamming; state transition count $\tau_b$ captures BLER on/off switching frequency.
\end{itemize}

A key insight from our real testbed measurements is that protocol-aware attacks are detectable through the \textit{combination} of multiple temporal features, not any single metric alone. While both protocol-aware attack windows and a majority of Normal windows exhibit near-zero BLER variance ($\sigma^2_b \approx 0$), the RBF-kernel SVM exploits joint differences in SINR statistics ($\mu_s$, $\sigma^2_s$) and CQI variability ($\sigma^2_c$) to achieve separation. Protocol-aware attacks subtly depress mean SINR ($\mu_s = 4.7$\,dB vs.\ $5.6$\,dB for Normal) and reduce CQI fluctuations ($\sigma^2_c \approx 1.2$ vs.\ $11.4$), even though these effects are invisible in per-sample analysis (Table~\ref{tab:kpm_stats}).

While Hachimi et al.~\cite{hachimi2020} used a per-sample KSVM as a binary false-negative recheck, our sliding-window approach captures temporal dynamics invisible to single-sample analysis. The KSVM (RBF kernel, $C$=0.5, $\gamma$=scale) is trained on a hybrid dataset combining real testbed sliding windows with synthetic windows (500 per class) generated from calibrated KPI profiles. A lower regularization ($C$=0.5 vs.\ the typical $C$=10) reduces overfitting to the specific SINR distribution of the training blocks, improving generalization across temporal non-stationarity. Evaluation uses blocked temporal 5-fold cross-validation: each mode's time-series is split into five consecutive blocks, with windows generated strictly within each block to prevent sample overlap between train and test sets.

\subsection{Stage 3: MobileNetV3 Protocol Classification}

Stage~3 is invoked only when Stage~1 detects a Deceptive attack or Stage~2 catches a false negative, keeping its overhead off the critical path for most samples. Raw I/Q snapshots (16,384 complex samples) are captured via passive USRP reception on the UE instance and converted to 224$\times$224 spectrograms using Welch's method (segment length 256, 78\% overlap), with power spectral density mapped to normalized dB scale.

MobileNetV3-Small~\cite{howard2019} (1.52M parameters) classifies these spectrograms into four protocol-aware sub-types: PSS/SSS (center-concentrated energy in 6--12~RBs), PDCCH (time-domain CORESET bursts in first 1--3~OFDM symbols), DMRS (comb-pattern at subcarrier intervals of 4--6), and Generic Deceptive (uniform broadband OFDM). Initial training uses 1,200 synthetic spectrograms (300 per class) generated from frequency-domain attack models, followed by fine-tuning with 200 real I/Q captures (50 per class) collected via full-duplex USRP~X310 passive reception during active jamming, bridging the sim-to-real domain gap as motivated by SenseORAN~\cite{reusmuns2024}.

\subsection{Detection Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:detection} compares Stage~1 alone versus the combined pipeline. Both stages are evaluated via blocked temporal 5-fold cross-validation on 1,492 samples: each mode's time-series is split into five consecutive blocks, sliding windows are generated strictly within each block, and each fold trains on four blocks (augmented with synthetic data) and tests on the held-out block. Stage~1 effectively detects Constant jamming (97.0\%) through sustained BLER elevation, and partially detects Random (68.9\%) and Reactive (65.6\%) through intermittent BLER spikes. However, protocol-aware attacks (Deceptive, PSS, PDCCH, DMRS) produce near-zero BLER and normal CQI (Table~\ref{tab:kpm_stats}), evading per-sample classification entirely---Stage~1 detects $<$1\% of these four modes.

The sliding-window KSVM in Stage~2 recovers nearly all false negatives. The key mechanism is multi-dimensional temporal analysis: although both protocol-aware attack windows and many Normal windows exhibit near-zero BLER variance, the RBF-kernel SVM ($C$=0.5) exploits joint differences in SINR statistics ($\mu_s$, $\sigma^2_s$) and CQI variability ($\sigma^2_c$) to achieve separation on cross-validated held-out blocks. Hybrid training with synthetic windows helps the model generalize across temporal variability. The combined pipeline achieves \textbf{100\% detection for six of seven modes}, with PDCCH at 91.9\% (limited by one temporal block where the narrow CORESET-targeted interference falls below the SVM's decision boundary), and 5.3\% combined false alarm rate.

Table~\ref{tab:latency} shows all classification paths remain well within the Near-RT RIC's 100\,ms budget, with the fastest path (ATTACK\_CONFIRMED) completing in under 1\,ms and the heaviest path (FN\_CAUGHT, requiring all three stages) at $\leq$23\,ms (p95). \subsection{Closed-Loop MLOps Validation}

We validated the complete closed-loop MLOps cycle on the testbed, extending Rahman et al.'s SAJD architecture~\cite{rahman2025} from binary detection to multi-class classification with three key enhancements:

\textbf{(1) GMM Labeler rApp:} The Labeler ingests raw KPI telemetry from InfluxDB in 5-minute sliding windows (batch size $N$=30), applies moving average smoothing (window=5), standardizes features, and fits a 2-component GMM for unsupervised Normal/Attack clustering. An Average Rate of Change (ARC) metric monitors concept drift; when $|\text{ARC}| > \tau = 0.0004$, the GMM is re-estimated. In our validation, the Labeler correctly stratified 30 samples into 21~Normal and 9~Attack labels, consistent with the known ground truth.

\textbf{(2) Training Manager rApp:} The Manager queries InfluxDB to compare xApp detection verdicts against GMM-generated labels, computing a rolling accuracy metric. When accuracy drops below 30\% (configurable threshold from~\cite{rahman2025}), it triggers automated Stage~1 MLP retraining. Our implementation supports both ClearML-based orchestration (task cloning, queue submission, model registry) and a standalone fallback mode that directly invokes training with real labeled data.

\textbf{(3) A1-Interface Model Deployment:} Upon retraining completion, the Training Manager sends a \texttt{MODEL\_UPDATE} message to the xApp via an A1-like HTTP interface (port 5000), carrying the updated model path and training metadata. The xApp performs atomic hot-reload: a background thread detects the update, acquires a threading lock, swaps the model's \texttt{state\_dict}, and resumes inference without service interruption. In our end-to-end validation, the full cycle---from KPI ingestion through GMM labeling, retraining, A1 notification, to hot-reload---completed within 5~minutes.

The observability layer consists of InfluxDB v2 for time-series storage of KPI measurements, detection verdicts, and GMM labels, with a Grafana dashboard providing real-time visualization of KPI trends, detection distributions, inference latency, and model accuracy.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented MSJC, a multi-stage jamming classification framework for 5G O-RAN that detects all seven jamming modes---including protocol-aware attacks targeting PSS, PDCCH, and DMRS---with 100\% detection for six of seven attack types (91.9\% for PDCCH), 5.3\% false alarm rate, and $\leq$23\,ms end-to-end latency (p95) on the CCI~xG Testbed, achieving 85\% Stage~3 protocol-aware sub-classification accuracy on real I/Q captures. All results are validated via blocked temporal 5-fold cross-validation with zero sample overlap between train and test sets, providing rigorous evidence beyond the in-sample or temporal hold-out evaluations common in prior work.

Our key technical insight is that protocol-aware attacks, while invisible to per-sample KPI classifiers, produce distinct multi-dimensional temporal signatures---including subtly depressed SINR and reduced CQI variability---that can be exploited through sliding-window statistical analysis with an RBF-kernel SVM. The three-stage cascade enables cost-effective deployment: most samples are classified in under 1\,ms by Stage~1, with the heavier Stage~3 spectrogram analysis invoked only when protocol-aware attacks are suspected.

Future work includes: (i)~multi-cell extension to detect spatially-distributed jamming, (ii)~active mitigation via E2SM-RC control actions (beam steering, power adaptation)~\cite{abdalla2022}, (iii)~sub-second KPM reporting through gNB scheduler modifications to improve temporal resolution, and (iv)~adversarial robustness against attackers who may adapt to our detection features~\cite{kakani2025}.
